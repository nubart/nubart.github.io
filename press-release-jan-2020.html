Press Release



An audio guide with no devices and no apps: After a successful career in Europe, Nubart, the revolutionary Audio Guide Visitors Keep, is launching in the USA.

With an attractive revenue share model for museums and venues with between 100,000 and 500,000 annual visitors, Nubart expects to achieve rapid growth in the highly fragmented American audio guide market.

Berlin, 8th January 2020 - Since the Stedelijk Museum in Amsterdam invented the audio-guides in 1952, there were only two ways of offering audio-guides in a museum: dedicated electronic devices or, more recently, smartphone apps.

Audio guide devices can be a relevant source of revenue for museums, but they are clunky and require maintenance. They generate waiting queues, need to be sanitized, have to be picked up again at the end of the tour and get broken every now and then. In short, they can be quite a source of trouble.

Digital apps (native smartphone apps or web-apps) make life definitely easier for the museum! But unfortunately, they don't make them richer. Audio guide web-apps are always free, as are 99 % of native apps. There is no return on investment and no source of income for a museum in an app.

If someone could make an audio guide digital, eliminating most logistical problems, but at the same time tangible, so that it can be sold at a profit, you would have found the perfect audio guide.

And this is exactly what the Barcelona-based company Nubart has achieved.

Nubart prints unique codes on a beautiful custom plastic card and makes them non-transferable thank's to its IP-protected LWAC-technology. Those cards can be easily sold to the visitor at the ticket counter: no big logistics nor extra staff required.


This delivery method allows visitors to stream the digital content (audios, images, videos and others) to their own smartphone, without having to download an app nor queue up to bring back any device. They can access the content not only at the exhibition itself, but also hours or days later comfortably seated at home with no crowds around. And with Nubart's integrated QR-code scanner, the access to the content could not be easier, even for digitally unsavvy people.


Tourists can keep the card as a cherished souvenir, which serves the international branding of the museum. “One museum even requested self-adhesive magnetic strips from us”, said Nubart’s CEO Rosa Sala, “so visitors can put the card on the fridge’s door! We found that very amusing”.

Since the cards provide a surface to print logos, something that is also not really feasible with devices or apps, the system is very attractive to sponsors.

A donation button can also be incorporated to the digital content of the audio guide.

"Since the cards can be sold, sponsored and also be a vehicle for donations, Nubart offers not one, but three potential sources of income to a museum", says Rosa Sala. "Visitors love the idea to be able to take the audio guide content home and keep listening comfortably even after their visit, so our take-up rate is high. Around 10-15 % on average when sold, and an amazing 65 % of usage when given for free as part of the admission fee. This definitely beats free audio guide apps, that rarely get more than 2 % of visitors, but also conventional audio guide devices, that even if offered for free rarely reach a 35 % of usage".

Nubart, with offices in Barcelona and Berlin, is a truly European company. But: "We've been wanting to expand across the pond for years, and in 2020 we are finally going to make it happen", says Sala. "We will be attending the AAM Annual Meeting in San Francisco this May, where we will meet suitable business partners to implement our innovative audio guide system and find our first customers while we work on establishing our American subsidiary Nubart International in the second half of 2020."

But why America? "American museums are flexible, market oriented and unbureaucratic. There is a very well established culture of giving in the US, and that is an advantage when looking for sponsorships in the cultural industry. This is a little different in Europe. We developed a revenue-share model purposely for the American market that would be difficult to apply widely in European museums. Our revenue-share targets museums in the mid range (100,000 to 500,000 visitors per year), a segment that is lucrative enough for us, but probably not enough for traditional audio guide companies dealing with devices. In this way we expect to reach a fast growth. But we also offer affordable pay-as-you-go packages for small museums, since we don't want an audio guide to be a luxury item for any museum, even if very small."

Nubart can collect a lot of relevant and anonymous information from the visitor’s smartphone and also includes a feedback-form. This enables even small museums to use big data to improve and to optimize the experience they provide to visitors, like their larger peers have been doing already for several years.

Last but not least: Nubart's cards are accessible for visually and hearing impaired people; they can be printed in eco-friendly materials; and they can be set in a highly innovative offline-mode for venues in areas with little or no connectivity.

Rosa Sala is not the typical entrepreneur: she has a PhD in Comparative Literature, speaks 5 languages and has 5 books published as an independent scholar before she decided to found Nubart in 2013. "I believe that Nubart also makes a lot of sense in sectors and venues where the implementation of traditional audio guides would be particularly complicated, such as sporting venues, hotel resorts, fairs or show rooms, and we are definitely going there! But given my background, my heart will always be very close to museums and cultural sites".

We wish her good luck at the other side of the pond!

Marcela Rosemberg
