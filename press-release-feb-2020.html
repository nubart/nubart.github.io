Press Release



A Barcelona-based company is launching in the US an innovation that will allow museums and other cultural venues to reduce costs and to avoid queues and waiting times by offering tickets and audio guides all at once, with one single card.

Berlin, February 5, 2020 - POS ticketing systems for museums and other cultural institutions are a veritable jungle. There is a confusing array of prices, costed extra items and payment mechanisms, all of which makes it almost impossible to compare directly different systems.

It may be a certein degree of hidden strategy behind this lack of clarity, since once a museum has signed a ticketing agreement with a supplier, it is not easy to break with it and to switch to another system, especially since ticketing is a 'mission-critical' tool that interfaces with other technologies. This is an ideal scenario to prune away some hidden costs.

On the other hand, we have the problem of the audio guides: many museums are inclined towards the so-called universal model, that includes the audio guide in the admission fee as a fundamental part of their service to the visitor. But by using dedicated devices that must be handed out and picked up, the waiting time per visitor is lengthened: you will have two lines, one for the ticket and another one to pick the free audio guide. Many visitors would rather give up the audio guide than stand in a line again.

The Barcelona-based company Nubart has set out to innovate by means of a ticketing system that will include for the first time non-transferable access to the audio guide directly from the visitor’s smartphone. In this way, there is only one queue, as the visitor only has to buy the Nubart ticket-card. Visitors can use the unique codes on the card to access the audio guide and take the card home as a souvenir. As the owner of the card, they can listen to the audio guide again whenever they want. At the same time, the museum ensures that only those who have paid for the ticket can enjoy this service.



The only cost that is understandably not included is the production of the audio guide content, warns Sala. "But the museum is not obliged to produce the content with us. In addition, there are already many museums that have content ready, perhaps included in an app or on old audio guide devices. We can adapt this content at no extra charge to our innovative delivery system". But if new content needs to be made, Nubart offers very attractive starter-packages that sponsor a significant part of its production. In some cases it is even possible to roll the cost for content production into the 'per visitor' fee on a revenue-share agreement.

And what about museums that are potentially interested, but tied by contract to another POS-ticketing supplier? "These museums can start by purchasing our non-transferable audio guide cards and selling them as an extra item. This way they will know how Nubart and our team work before taking such a step. Later, when the museum considers it appropriate, we can reconvert these same cards to our ticketing system, so nothing is wasted".

We wish Nubart much luck in the US with this innovation, which is already being successfully employed by several European venues.


Marcela Rosemberg
