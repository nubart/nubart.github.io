GPS Trigger Lines vs. Radius Circles: Why Fixed-Route Vehicles Need Directional Triggering


When an operator first configures a GPS-triggered audio guide, the choice of trigger zone type rarely receives much attention. A circle around each point of interest seems the natural shape: draw a radius, set a distance, and audio plays when the device enters the zone. For walking tours, this is perfectly adequate. For sightseeing boats, buses, and tourist trains following a fixed route, it produces a specific set of problems that only become visible once the system is running in practice.

This article explains what those problems are, how directional trigger lines address them, and — equally important — when radius-based triggers remain the better choice.

How Radius-Based Triggers Work

A radius-based GPS trigger defines a circular geofence around a point of interest. When the GPS device — whether a passenger's smartphone or a dedicated operator device — enters that circle, the audio track assigned to that point plays. The mechanism is simple and, for many use cases, sufficient.

The configuration choice that shapes real-world performance is the radius size. Set it too large, and adjacent trigger zones begin to overlap on routes where points of interest are closely spaced. A boat approaching a medieval bridge may trigger commentary for the palace behind it while the bridge is still several hundred metres ahead. Set the radius too small to avoid this, and a different problem emerges: a vessel moving at normal operating speed may pass through the entire zone in fewer GPS polling cycles than the system needs to register entry, and the trigger is missed entirely. The passenger hears nothing.

Both failure modes are common in practice. Operators who discover missed triggers often respond by enlarging the zones, which then causes early and overlapping playback. There is no radius value that cleanly solves all three constraints simultaneously on a route with closely spaced stops.

The Return Route Problem

On fixed-route vehicles — and particularly on harbour and river boats that complete a round trip — the radius trigger has a further structural problem: it fires in both directions.

Consider a harbour tour where the vessel leaves the dock, passes a lighthouse, completes a circuit of the bay, and returns past the same lighthouse before berthing again. A radius zone around the lighthouse triggers when the vessel enters it, regardless of which direction the vessel is heading. On the outbound leg, the commentary plays correctly. On the return, the same track plays again — over the same passengers, who heard it an hour earlier.

Operators typically handle this by either accepting the repeated playback, disabling commentary on the return leg entirely, or configuring shorter tracks for the return that feel less intrusive. None of these is a satisfying solution, and all require ongoing manual management. A route with eight points of interest and a return leg means sixteen potential trigger events to manage, rather than eight.

How Directional Trigger Lines Solve This

A directional trigger line is a virtual line drawn across the waterway or road at a specific point on the route. Audio fires when the vehicle crosses the line — and because the line has an assigned direction, the system knows which way the crossing occurred.

The practical consequences of this difference are significant. Speed sensitivity is reduced: the trigger fires at the moment of line crossing rather than on sustained presence within a zone, so a vessel moving quickly through a narrow strait is no more likely to miss a trigger than one proceeding slowly through open water. Overlap between adjacent triggers is eliminated by geometry rather than by radius calibration: two parallel lines will never overlap regardless of how close the corresponding points of interest are.

The return route problem disappears entirely. A trigger line can be configured to fire only in the outbound direction, so the same track does not play again when the vessel returns past the same point. Or — and this is where the approach becomes genuinely useful — the line can be set to fire in both directions independently, with different audio assigned to each crossing. The outbound track describes the lighthouse as it approaches. The inbound track, heard on the return, offers something different: the history of the dock the vessel is heading back towards, or simply silence. The operator decides; the system enforces it reliably.

A Concrete Example: A Harbour Tour

Take a vessel that departs from a central quay, passes a castle on the headland, crosses a harbour mouth, rounds a small island, and returns via the same route. Five points of interest, each passed twice.

With radius-based triggers, the operator faces a matrix of decisions: how large to set each zone without overlapping neighbours, whether to suppress return-leg playback and if so how, and what to do about the harbour mouth — a narrow passage where the vessel will be moving at speed and where a small zone is likely to produce missed triggers. Each configuration choice is a compromise, and any change to the vessel's operating speed or route requires recalibration.

With directional trigger lines, each point of interest gets two lines: one facing the outbound direction, one facing inbound. Five points of interest produce ten line definitions. Each line fires once per crossing, in the correct direction, at the correct moment. Outbound and return tracks are managed independently. Vessel speed has no effect. No zone calibration is needed.

The configuration takes slightly longer to set up initially. Once in place, it requires no ongoing adjustment unless the route itself changes.

Radius Circles vs. Trigger Lines: A Summary

Property Radius circles Directional lines
Trigger precision Depends on radius size and vessel speed Fires at the moment of crossing, speed-independent
Return route handling Fires again on return unless suppressed manually Direction-aware; different audio or silence assignable per direction
Overlap between adjacent stops Risk increases on closely spaced routes No overlap by geometry
Speed sensitivity High-speed passage through small zones may miss trigger Minimal; crossing event registered on entry
Configuration complexity Simple initial setup; ongoing calibration often needed More initial definition; stable once configured
Best suited for Walking tours, free-roaming guides Boats, buses, trains on fixed routes

When Radius-Based Triggers Remain the Right Choice

It would be misleading to suggest that directional lines are always superior. They are not: they are the right choice for vehicles on fixed routes, and the wrong choice for contexts where direction of approach is unpredictable.

A walking audio guide through a historic city centre is the clearest counterexample. A visitor approaching a cathedral might come from the north, the east, or diagonally across a square. A directional line would work only for visitors crossing it from a specific angle; a visitor approaching from the opposite side would hear nothing. A radius zone fires regardless of approach direction, which is exactly the correct behaviour for a free-roaming visitor.

This is why Nubart uses radius-based triggers for its static audio guide product, where visitors explore at their own pace and in their own sequence. The two approaches serve different contexts, and the distinction matters when evaluating any GPS audio commentary system for a specific operation.

What Happens When GPS Signal Drops

Neither trigger line nor radius circle can fire when there is no GPS signal. Routes that pass through tunnels, under low bridges, or along river sections with poor satellite coverage will experience trigger failures regardless of zone type. This is an inherent limitation of GPS-based positioning.

The practical solution for fixed-route vehicles is hybrid triggering: GPS-based firing for the majority of the route, with time-triggered sequences as a fallback for defined dead zones. The operator marks which segments of the route have unreliable positioning; the system advances through the corresponding tracks on a timed schedule rather than waiting for a GPS event that may not arrive. Combined with content preloading — so audio tracks are already on passenger devices before the vessel enters an area with no connectivity — this approach produces consistent playback even on routes where signal conditions are imperfect.
